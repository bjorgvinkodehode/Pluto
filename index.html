Pluto


Pluto is a dwarf planet

Pluto is a dwarf planet located in the Kuiper Belt, a region of icy bodies beyond Neptune’s orbit. It was discovered in 1930 by Clyde Tombaugh and was considered the ninth planet in our solar system until 2006 when it was reclassified as a dwarf planet by the International Astronomical Union (IAU). Pluto is named after the Roman god of the underworld and is known for its unique characteristics that set it apart from other planets.

Pluto’s discovery

Pluto was discovered by Clyde Tombaugh on February 18, 1930, at Lowell Observatory in Flagstaff, Arizona. It was named by an 11-year-old girl from Oxford, England named Venetia Burney. Pluto’s status as a planet has been debated since its discovery due to its small size and unusual orbit. In 2006, the IAU reclassified Pluto as a “dwarf planet” because it did not meet all three criteria for being a planet

Pluto’s physical characteristics

Pluto is about two-thirds the size of Earth’s moon and has a rocky core surrounded by a mantle of water ice and other frozen volatiles such as nitrogen and methane. Its surface is covered in frozen nitrogen, carbon monoxide, and methane with mountains made of water ice that can reach up to 11,000 feet high.

Pluto’s moons

Pluto has five known moons: Charon, Nix, Hydra, Kerberos, and Styx. Charon is the largest moon and is about half the size of Pluto itself. It was discovered in 1978 by James Christy and named after his wife Charlene. The other four moons were discovered between 2005 and 2012 by astronomers using the Hubble Space Telescope.

Pluto’s current status and future exploration

Pluto remains an object of fascination for astronomers and space enthusiasts alike due to its unique characteristics and history. In recent years, NASA’s New Horizons spacecraft has provided new insights into Pluto’s geology and atmosphere during its flyby in July 2015. Future missions to Pluto are being planned to explore this fascinating world further.
